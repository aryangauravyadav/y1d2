What's the point of making good decisions?

Dec 21, 2022

At times I am in the spirit of trying to understand my intuitions. What follows is me complicating an answer to a simple question.

'What's the point of making good decisions?'. My intuitive response is, 'if you make good decisions, you'll end up with good outcomes'. There are, of course, situations where making good decisions doesn't lead you to good outcomes. But for the most part, I think my intuitive response works, and I shall try to back it up.

i. Bertha

Bertha is incandescent about her one goal in life; she wants to write 10,000 words every day! Every second spent not writing is a second Bertha thinks is wasted. Decisions like what to eat or wear have become inconsequential (or, at the very least, an afterthought) to her.

One morning, Bertha wakes up to grey clouds outside her bedroom window. She pays little attention to it and gets on with her day. As Bertha leaves her apartment, she tries to call for a taxi to take her to the library. To her chagrin, none show up, so Bertha decides to walk instead. Lo and behold, five minutes in, raindrops from the grey clouds start trickling down on her; Bertha realises she left her umbrella at home. She could go back to her apartment but chooses not to. She thought the droplets were merely signs of a drizzle, and she was in no mood to waste any more time. Alas, Bertha was wrong; heavy showers began pouring on her.

The next day, poor Bertha catches a fever because of the rain, which leaves her unable to write.

Let's say we bring in a group of people to assess what happened to Bertha and get a 50:50 split between them. On one side, let's call them the 'serves her right' group, thinks Bertha had this coming to her. On the other side, we have the 'not to blame' group who argue that what happened to Bertha wasn't her fault because she couldn't have foreseen all the events that unfolded.

ii. 'Serves her right'

What do the arguments for this side look like?

A1 - Bertha is too caught up in her aspiration to write down 10,000 words every day.

A2 - Instead, she should pay more attention to the world around her.

C1 - If she had paid more attention to the world, she wouldn't have caught a fever.

C2 - Bertha got what was coming to her. This serves her right.

A1 + A2 = C1

C1 → C2

C1 seems to reason along the same lines as my earlier intuition; good decisions lead to good outcomes. Paying more attention to the world (a good decision) would have prevented her from getting a fever (good outcome).

So, is C1 correct?

What were some things that happened in Bertha's world?

W1 - Bertha woke up to grey clouds in the sky.
W2 - A taxi didn't arrive, so Bertha walked to the library.
W3 - Raindrops started to fall five minutes into her walk.
W4 - Heavy showers began eventually.
W5 (the bad outcome) - Bertha catches a fever1.

From W1 to W4, Bertha could have acted differently to prevent W5 from happening. For example:

W1 → G1

If Bertha noticed the clouds instead of rushing into her day, she might have checked the weather forecast or carried an umbrella before leaving her apartment.

W2 → G2

Bertha could have gone back up to her apartment and grabbed her umbrella before she decided to walk.

So, if Bertha had made good decisions like G1 or G2, she could have avoided W5. So arguably, it's reasonable to say that C1 is correct.

So then, what's the takeaway if C1 is right?

If C1 is correct, you have an answer to what the point of making good decisions is. If you want a good outcome, like, being able to write 10,000 words every day, then making good decisions that lead you to it is instrumentally valuable.

iii. 'Not to blame'

What do the arguments for this side look like?

A1 - Bertha might have been too caught up in her aspiration of writing 10,000 words every day.

A2 - But she could not have possibly foreseen everything that happened to her that day.

D1 - Since Bertha couldn't have foreseen her getting a fever from events W1 to W4, it would be wrong to blame her.

A1 + A2 = D1

Prima facie, D1 seems to serve as a rebuttal to C1, but I don't think this is right. D1 adds nuance to C1.

It is plausible that Bertha couldn't have foreseen that a taxi would not arrive when she called for one (W2). Of course, much like Bertha, we all deal with uncertainty all the time and no decision we take is done without it.

Uncertainty is a property of decision-making, and a good decision takes that into account.

So again,

W1 → G1

Bertha notices the clouds and can check the weather forecast. Bertha has no way of knowing it will rain until it does, but she errs on the side of caution and carries an umbrella with her.
